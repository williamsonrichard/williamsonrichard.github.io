Cervantes and the printing of Don Quixote

Introduction

In 1604, on the 11th of September in Valladolid, Antonio de Herrera y Tordesillas wrote, in response to a memorial pertaining to the novel Don Quixote submitted to the Consejo de Castilla (Council of Castile) by Miguel de Cervantes, that:

se le podrá dar licencia para imprimille [a license to print it should be granted].

These two records — Cervantes’ memorial and Herrera’s report — are of rather complex provenance, and fit into a rich context. We shall explore here how RiC and a couple of complementary standards can express aspects of this.

Councils

By the time Cervantes wrote his memorial, the kingdoms of Castile and Aragon, though still to a large degree distinct, had had a single monarch since the marriage of Queen Isabella I of Castile and King Ferdinand II of Aragon in the 15th century. In addition to making up roughly the mainland of modern Spain, the kingdoms — the one or the other — controlled what is now referred to as the Spanish empire (including much of the Americas and considerable parts of Europe).

Governance was through entities known as councils (consejos): one each originally for Castile and Aragon, these pre-dating the unification of their crowns. Gradually numerous councils were split off from these two, being given responsibility either for a particular territory, for example the Council of the Indies (Consejo de las Indias) — which we shall return to later — or for a particular function, for example the Council of Finance (Consejo de Hacienda), both with origins in the Council of Castile. Councils were also created from scratch, the most important of which had both kingdoms in their remit, for example Council of State (Consejo de Estado).

That Herrera was in Vallodolid places our two records at a quite specific point in time: the royal court in Castile, to which the Council of Castile was attached, had been in Madrid for several decades until 1601, and moved to Valladolid only from 1601 to 1606, whereupon it moved permanently back to Madrid. This event is of archival significance because, in conjunction with the move, a transfer of some records of the Council of Castile to Simancas was made, along with records of certain of the other councils.

Simancas

Let us pause to discuss the General Archive of Simancas (Archivo General de Simancas) a little; it is of first-rank importance both for its holdings and for its history. By default, one would typically regard an archive as a Corporate Body (RiC-E11), namely as an institution working with archival material; in this sense, the General Archive of Simancas was founded around sixty years before Cervantes’ request, as we shall discuss in more detail below. Secondly, it usually has a physical location, Place (RiC-E22) in RiC, and may be identified with it; in the case of the General Archive of Simancas, its purpose-built location within the pre-existing Castle of Simancas is regarded as having being completed in the 1580s. Thirdly, one might regard it as (an enormous!) Record Set (RiC-E03), i.e. identify it with its collection of records. Such a point of view goes beyond the boundaries of some traditional points of view upon archival description which might have fonds as the broadest entity, but has been made use of in practice long before RiC — we shall touch upon an example later — and will be useful to us.

The date given in the diagram for the founding of the General Archive of Simancas comes from a royal decree of the 16th of September 1540. This record stipulates that certain documents be sent to one Juan Mosquera de Molina, who — this is the crucial point, Simancas not being mentioned explicitly — was an official (alcaide: commandant/warden) at the Castle of Simancas. Included is the nuance that the decree was actually written (apart from the king’s signature) by Juan Vázquez de Molina, in Flanders as it happens (though we do not include this in the diagram!).

Records of the Council of Castile I

Although both diagrams above refer, for simplicity, simply to the Collection of the Council of Castile, and although this Record Set is referred to in existing archival descriptions as a fonds, it actually encompasses only a small proportion of the archived records of the Council of Castile. Apart from those which were sent, as mentioned earlier, to Simancas in 1606, others, mainly older and including those specified in the founding royal decree of 1540, were organised, and it seems largely collected by the industry of, Diego de Ayala, who by another royal decree was installed as an archivist at the Castle of Simancas in 1561 (initially along with a second archivist, but the latter died in 1563) — this work was of invaluable importance! More or less everything else (which was not lost) travelled to Madrid with the royal court.

Any modelling in RiC, such as that expressed by the above diagram, is to be understood as having an ‘open-world semantics’: more information could always be added to it, no matter how high a degree of nuance and accuracy might be achieved. Here we could for example include details of the royal decree mandating Diego de Ayala’s position of head archivist at Simancas. Or if we were to obtain more details on the accruals to Simancas, for example those created after 1606, further Record Sets could be added.

Technical points of note in the diagram include the following.

  1. The distinction between has end date (RO71i) and has or had all members with creation date (R081i) for a Record Set. Archival descriptions have often been ambiguous about which is meant: a Record Set might well have been ‘open’ for a period of time — for example as long as an organisation existed if the Record Set documents the activities of that organisation — since the last record belonging to it was created. With regard to the diagram, the fact that all of the records of the Council of Castile held at Simancas were created between 1437 and 1648 comes from PARES; whereas we have not researched when the last record arranged by Diego de Ayala was created, but the set of such records certainly ends with his death. In general, has or had all members with creation date or one of its variants (has or had most members with creation date (R083i) for instance), can always be used, even if the end date of the specified period time is not exact, but it is preferable to be precise where one can.

  2. Free use has been made of the Record Set entity as an intellectual abstraction. A Record Set indeed, unlike a Record (RiC-E04), is not required in RiC to have an Instantiation (RiC-E06), and one reason for this is precisely so as to allow for its usage in expressing context, as in the diagram; the records arranged by Diego de Ayala, for example, do all have a physical incarnation, and sub-groups of them are physically stored together in Ayala’s arrangement, but the diagram does not require or imply that the entirety of these records are stored physically together, or even identifiable as a single entity in the storage metadata. On the other hand, a Record Set certainly can be regarded as having an Instantiation, this being the case in PARES for the totality records of the Council at Castile held at Simancas, for example.

  3. A couple of the Activities in the diagram are specified as having an Activity Type corresponding to fundamental archival processes: Arrangement and Accrual. Classifying by means of Activity Type in this way is important both for inter-operability and for allowing orientation of data around specific aspects of archival work, without privileging any of them. Canonical vocabularies for Activity Type, and other attributes, are planned for RiC as a supplement to RiC-CM and RiC-O.

    In the diagram, Event is used in one case, and Activity in all the others. RiC-CM gives the difference between the two as follows.

    An Activity is an agent designed and performed event that has an intended purpose or purposes.

    The Event in the diagram, the movement of the royal court to Madrid, had archival consequences, but these can hardly be said to have been part of the reason for the move; whereas all of the Activities in the diagram specifically pertain to archives, and a definite archival purpose.

  4. As is almost always the case in RiC, a property can be used more than once, as has or had location (RiC-R075i) is in the diagram. The philosophy is one of facilitating discovery: somebody encountering that the fact that the Habsburg royal court is described as having both Valladolid and Madrid as locations would, if it is relevant for them, be prompted to dig more deeply into the context to understand why both are listed (that the royal was court, that this had archival ramifications, and so on).

    Whilst we did not include this in the diagram, RiC allows for decoration of relations by means of certain relation attributes. For instance, Date of Relation (RiC-RA02) could certainly be used in conjunction with has or had location to express that the court was at Valladolid from 1601 to 1606, and similarly for Madrid. RiC-O makes use of a technical device known as an n-ary relation class — factoring of an object property through an individual for which properties such as a Date can be defined — to allow for relation attributes; it could look as follows in this case, where rico:PlaceRelation is the n-ary relation class, and the relation is associated with place (R074i), of which has or had location is a sub-relation, becomes a ‘shortcut’ for the path consisting of the two horizontal arrows (we mean this only informally here, but it is also true in a formal sense in RiC-O, albeit one more arrow is needed to express it — we shall not go into this here).

Records of the Council of Castile II

Cervantes’ memorial and Herrera’s report were amongst those records which moved with the royal court to Madrid. Literature was censored in Habsburg Spain at the time, and requests for licenses for printing were handled by Escribanías de Cámara y Justicia (Notaries of the Chamber and Justice); over the next couple of centuries the records remained at the part of court handling matters of justice. The Ancien Régime gradually fell, and the Council of Castile ceased to exist in 1834; the Tribunal Supremo (Supreme Court), enshrined in Spain’s constitution of 1812, took over its role with respect to justice, and in particular as gatekeeper of records pertaining to justice.

It would take us too far afield to delve into the details, but suffice it to say that the Council of Castile’s records at court fell over the years into a state of a certain degree of neglect. The need for their archival was recognised, but Simancas no longer had space to receive records; omitting the details of various earlier alleviations, the Archivo Histórico Nacional (National Historical Archive) was constructed in the second half of the nineteenth century, and eventually (between 1896 and 1899), the fonds of the Council of Castile held at the Supreme Court (and elsewhere if not at Simancas) were sent there. Specifically, Cervantes’ memorial and Herrera’s report belonged to a series already compiled by the 1730s, known as the Archivo Antiguo de Consejo. Consultas y Decretos (Ancient Archive of the Council. Consultations and Decrees).

Extraction of Cervantes’ memorial

This is not quite the end of the story when it comes to the provenance of Cervantes’ memorial and Herrera’s report. In the 1980s, archivists at Archivo Histórico Nacional extracted into what is regarded as a new Record Set, Colección de pergaminos y documentos del Consejo de Castilla, certain records of the Council of Castile whose instantiations were regarded as particularly vulnerable from a conservation or display point of view. Cervantes’ memorial and Herrera’s report were amongst these.

As is drawn attention to in PARES, the creation of this Record Set a priori did not adhere to the principle of preservation of organic provenance (although it should be noted that much in this regard had already been lost by the time of the transfer to Archivo Histórico Nacional — the situation was not necessarily much ameliorated, it seems, by the arrangement of the records at the archive following the move). RiC’s concept of a Record Set, though, fully encompasses a collection defined according to these criteria, and the decentralised nature of RiC means that the provenance can be as accurately expressed as in a traditional hierarchical example.

Given that RiC makes a careful distinction between record resources (in their three guises) and their instantiations, one might at first balk a little at our referring to ‘extraction of records’: is not the Cervantes memorial the same intellectually as it always was, and the extraction purely a matter of the storage and handling of its original instantiation? The key point is that a record as an intellectual entity is more than the substance of its contents; all aspects of its provenance, and context more generally as far as it is relevant archivally, are a part of it too. That certain instantiations of a record are deemed of such vulnerability as for this to be of relevance for an archivist in working with it (making it available to the public, for instance) is certainly a judgement of sufficient weight as to justify introducing an intellectual entity (Record Set) which specifically aggregrates them, as the Archivo Histórico Nacional did. From a practical point of view, one has the possibility to introduce an Instantiation of this new Record Set, which would be a natural entity to which to tie details of how one handles the vulnerabilities of the records.

Cultural heritage aspects

RiC’s domain extends to the content of records in a direct way only in so far as this has immediate archival implications: the subject or subjects of a record, for instance, are likely to be of importance (to enable the record to be discovered), but broader intellectual interpretation or reflection would typically be out of scope archivally. RiC fully allows, though, to pass in a structured way to the broader cultural heritage domain.

Cervantes’ memorial, for instance, contains his signature. This is significant for several reasons, not least with regard to questions of authenticity. We can highlight its importance by expressing it as a Record Part (RiC-E05), but to more specifically indicate its nature, we can view it as a Mark (E37) as defined in the cultural heritage standard CIDOC CRM. To enable this, we view Cervantes’ memorial as a Document (E31) in the CIDOC sense, in addition to as a Record in RiC; view Cervantes himself as an Actor (E39) in the CIDOC sense, in addition to as a Person in RiC; and make use of the CIDOC properties shows visual item (P65) and is identified by (P1). One could go much further in this direction!

Records pertaining to the ransoming of Cervantes

Cervantes crops up in the archives of the various Councils in different ways, not only in the writing of the memorial on the printing of Don Quixote. Thus he becomes a relation between different fonds which would be lost in hierarchical points of view on archival description. We turn now to exploring one example of this, and to how RiC allows us to formulate it.

Cervantes had an eventful life! In the early 1570s he served as a soldier, but was captured at sea, along with his brother, by pirates in 1575, and held for ransom in Algiers for several years thereafter. Cervantes’ mother, Leonor de Cortinas worked to free her sons; amongst other records of her endeavours, she submitted a memorial to the Council of War (one of the territory-wide councils which took form in the 1500s) in 1578, requesting merchandise of value 8000 ducats towards paying off the ransom, in view of Cervantes’ known good service. We are unsure if the memorial itself exists in the archives at Simancas, or is known only by reference (in which case it is a good example of how a Record can be relevant for an archival description, even when an Instantiation of it may not be held by any of the relevant archival institutions), but the Council of War’s response granting 2000 ducats at least is held there.

The archives of the Council of the Indies, held at the Archivo General de Indias, meanwhile contain the Record Set Expediente sobre los méritos y servicios de Miguel de Cervantes Saavedra, two of the records of which date from 1578 and are directly related, in different ways, to the question of Cervantes’ release. One is based upon information provided by Cervantes’ father, Rodrigo de Cervantes. Incidentally, this Record Set (file) would appear to be another example where a different principle of organisation than organic provenance has been followed in the archiving of it; being able to place it in a broader context as we do here allows to break free of the choices made in that archiving, whilst remaining faithful to the reality of its existence. This is equally so at a slightly higher level: whilst one of the four records in this Record Set (we will not consider it otherwise here) would naturally have been the domain of the Council of the Indies, it is not immediately obvious that this was the case for the two records we are interested in!

Beyond the traditional archival context

The use of archives — in any form: in legal matters, for research, … — situates the archival domain in a very broad context. Whilst this is not itself within RiC’s scope, RiC does allow for building a bridge to it, as we shall now explore in a couple of examples.

In Spain, a project resulting in an online catalogue, MetaPARES, was carried out a few years to link archival material to scholarly documents citing it. Whilst the project is at the time of writing not fully realised, there are already quite a number of entries pertaining to Cervantes, for example.

One of them concerns an article from 2016 bringing to light new biographical details on Cervantes, these coming from certain records held at the Archivo General de Indias. The article contains within it transcriptions of these records. By bringing this article into our archival description context as we do below, we allow for discovery of these transcriptions, and thus in the end the archival context, not only the broader cultural heritage one, is enriched.

Here, in addition to RiC, we have made use of the Bibliographic Ontology, Dublin Core, and the RDF schema. A few points of note:

  1. We have modelled the transcription of the record included in the diagram only as an Instantiation. As RiC-CM indicates, it is a matter of judgement whether an Instantiation derived from another can be regarded as an instantiation of a new Record (as well as being an instantiation of the original one). A transcription typically would be regarded as giving rise to a new Record, as significant intellectual endeavour may well be involved in a transcription, and recording of the context of that endeavour is then important archivally (allowing for the exploration of questions of bias, for instance). In the setting of the diagram, though, the transcription is only included within the text of the research article in question, and as such not quite within the archival domain as such, for which Record in the RiC sense should be reserved.
  2. We see how Thing (RiC-E01) is useful to make the bridge between RiC and other domains: although one should always use a more specific entity within the archival domain, several relations in RiC allow for Thing in their domain, range, or both, and, as in the diagram, it can be very useful to use these relations in cases where only one of the domain and the range are archival entities.
  3. The diagram illustrates how RiC allows for the archival domain to make contact with broader conceptions of Document: from a bibliographic point of view, the cited Record in question is no more than any other Document, but from an archival point of view it is essential to know that it is something more!

We give one more example. The intellectual Dámaso Alonso y Fernández de las Redondas studied various records in the reading rooms of the Archivo Histórico Nacional, his research, as in many such cases, leading to the record Expediente de investigación del Archivo Histórico Nacional correspondiente a Dámaso Alonso Fernández de las Redondas (Research file of the Archivo Histórico Nacional relating to Dámaso Alonso Fernández de las Redondas). Because of this and his relation to several other records, Dámaso Alonso is recorded as an agent within PARES.

Not apparent from the PARES description, though, is the fact that Alonso was also a noted Cervantes scholar, for example writing the essay “Oscillation in the character of Sancho” in 1969. By bringing this into the context, we again open for discovery of influences that may be of archival or literary significance.
