Two competing entities dependent upon a third

Model

  1. Three entities: F, T_1, T_2.
  2. F ('farmer') produces a product P_F.
  3. Both T_1 and T_2 ('technicians') produce the same product P_T in single quantities.
  4. P_F is necessary for T_1's survival in quantity S, and the same for T_2.
  5. P_T is not necessary for F's survival, and F is interested in at most one delivery of P_T per model cycle.
  6. P_F can be produced in quantity Q at intervals of duration D_F, and no more than necessary will be produced.
  7. P_T can be delivered to F at intervals of duration D_T_1 (resp. D_T_2) by T_1 (resp. T_2).

Stability

Permanent survival of both T_1 and T_2 can occur only under the following conditions:

  1. Repeated delivery of P_T is desirable for F.
  2. Q is greater than or equal to 2S.
  3. T_1 and T_2 require exactly the same quantity of P_F for a delivery of P_T.
  4. At least one of D_T_1 and D_T_2 is less than or equal to D_F, and the other is less than or equal to 2D_F (it is sufficient that both are less than or equal to 2D_F after the first model cycle, or if F is willing to wait two cycles for the first delivery)

Indeed, under these conditions, F can deliver P_F in quantity 2S to T_1 (resp. or T_2) in one cycle in exchange for P_T, and deliver P_F in quantity 2S to T_2 (resp. T_1) in the next cycle in exchange for P_T, continuing to alternate in this way.

Permanent survival of one of T_1 and T_2 can occur only under the following conditions:

  1. Repeated delivery of P_T is desirable for F.
  2. Q is greater than or equal to S.
  3. At least one of D_T_1 and D_T_2 is less than or equal to D_F.

Indeed, under these conditions, F can deliver P_F in quantity S to whichever of T_1 and T_2 requires a smaller quantity of P_F in exchange for a delivery of P_T, assuming that this quantity is greater than or equal to S, and that whichever of D_T_1 and D_T_2 is relevant is less than or equal to D_F. Whichever of T_1 and T_2 requires a larger quantity of P_F, assuming that both meet the delivery time requirement, will not survive.

If the cheaper of T_1 and T_2 cannot meet the delivery time requirement, it is possible that neither will survive, even if the more expensive of the two can meet the delivery time requirement: it depends upon the strength of the desirability of P for F, as well as F's evaluation of an acceptable cost of quicker delivery.

Verdict

Survival even of one of T_1 and T_2 is even more fragile than in the case that there is only one 'technician'. In addition to the factors mentioned in that case, either:
  1. An extremely high degree of co-operation between T_1 and T_2 is necessary to ensure equal 'pricing' of P_T; F must produce double as much of P_F; and the logistics of carrying out the alternating deliveries of P_T are more complicated; or
  2. 'Competition' between T_1 and T_2 to lower price leads to uncertainty as to which will survive, as well as the possibility that neither will survive, if the price is driven below S (noting that it is unlikely that S can be estimated precisely).

Variants

  1. If, instead of one 'farmer' F, there are two 'farmers' F_1 and F_2, there are a few possibilities. If both F_1 and F_2 are aware of both T_1 and T_2, or F_1 and F_2 are aware of the price the other is paying for P_T (or they co-operate in insisting upon a common price), the model is not significantly altered, and moreover is not significantly altered if further farmers are present: as in the case that there is only one 'technician' but more than one 'farmer', the survival of one or both of T_1 and T_2 over a larger number of cycles becomes more likely due to relaxation of the desirability condition, but ultimate survival is not significantly more likely.

    Otherwise the model reduces either to two independent instances of the case that there is only one 'technician' and one 'farmer', or to one instance of the latter and one of the one-farmer-to-two-technicians case. It is then possible that T_1 and T_2 will both survive, but this this survival is dependent upon a highly fragile lack of awareness/common knowledge/co-operation between F_1 and F_2.

  2. Variants 2. and 3. of the case that there is only one 'technician' and one 'farmer' apply essentially unchanged here.
  3. If one of T_1 or T_2 is able to 'innovate' to create a different product which is desirable for F, the 'competition' aspect of survival can be circumnavigated, and the possibility opens for reduction to two independent instances of the case that there is only one 'technician' and one 'farmer'.

Effect of regulation

  1. F could be required through regulation to produce P_F in quantity 2S at intervals of duration D_F, and to alternate in delivery to T_1 and T_2. The ability of F to produce P_F in sufficient quantities in time is unlikely to be able to regulated, however. In addition, the regulation of the logistiscs of alternating delivery may be complicated.

  2. T_1 and T_2 could be required through regulation to 'sell at the same price' (require the same quantity of P_F for a delivery of T). This removes the possibility of competition between T_1 and T_2 and ensures that either both or neither survives, but overall may make survival of neither more likely, due to the greater demands upon F and the logistic complexity of alternating delivery.

    The common price at which T_1 and T_2 are required to sell might or might not be required by regulation; it does not affect the likelihood of survival significantly. Regulating it will likely avoid the situation that a price less than S is agreed, but since delivery of P_T is subject to its desirability for F, it is of little consequence for T_1 and T_2's survival whether no deal is made due to too high a price being prescribed through regulation, or whether too low a price is agreed.
